Simulating the Poisson Counting Process

From discrete approximation to continuous-time stochastic processes — Daniele De Pascali 1984462

Introduction to Counting Processes

A counting process \( \{N(t), t \geq 0\} \) is a stochastic process that represents the total number of events that have occurred by time \( t \). These processes are fundamental in modeling random arrivals, failures, requests, or any phenomenon where discrete events occur over continuous time.

Defining Properties of a Counting Process
  1. Initial condition: \( N(0) = 0 \) (no events at time zero)
  2. Non-negative integers: \( N(t) \in \{0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots\} \) for all \( t \geq 0 \)
  3. Non-decreasing: If \( s < t \), then \( N(s) \leq N(t) \)
  4. Right-continuous: The process has right-continuous sample paths with left limits
  5. Increments: For \( s < t \), \( N(t) - N(s) \) represents the number of events in interval \( (s, t] \)

Examples in cybersecurity and beyond: Network packet arrivals, intrusion attempts, login requests, server failures, customer arrivals, radioactive decay events, and cyberattack occurrences all follow counting processes.

Simulation Approach: Discrete Approximation

To simulate a continuous-time counting process over interval \( [0, T] \) with average rate \( \lambda \), we employ a discrete approximation method that divides time into small subintervals.

Step Description Mathematical Expression
1. Discretize Divide \( [0, T] \) into \( n \) subintervals Width \( \Delta t = \frac{T}{n} \)
2. Probability Event probability in each subinterval \( p = \frac{\lambda \Delta t}{1} = \frac{\lambda T}{n} \)
3. Generate For each subinterval, generate Bernoulli trial \( X_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p) \)
4. Count Cumulative sum gives total events by time \( t \) \( N(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{\lfloor nt/T \rfloor} X_i \)

Key insight: As \( n \to \infty \) (subintervals become infinitesimally small), this discrete approximation converges to a continuous-time Poisson process. For practical simulation, \( n = 5000 \) provides excellent approximation for \( T = 1 \).

Why this works: In a small time interval \( \Delta t \), the probability of an event is approximately \( \lambda \Delta t \), and the probability of multiple events is negligible (\( o(\Delta t) \)). This captures the essence of a Poisson process.

The Poisson Process: Theoretical Foundation

The simulation approximates a Poisson process, one of the most important stochastic processes in probability theory. A Poisson process with rate \( \lambda \) is characterized by three fundamental properties.

Defining Properties of a Poisson Process

Property 1: Independent Increments

The numbers of events in disjoint time intervals are independent. If \( (t_1, t_2] \) and \( (t_3, t_4] \) are disjoint intervals, then \( N(t_2) - N(t_1) \) and \( N(t_4) - N(t_3) \) are independent random variables.

Property 2: Stationary Increments

The distribution of events in an interval depends only on the length of the interval, not its location. For any \( s, t \geq 0 \), \( N(t+s) - N(s) \) has the same distribution as \( N(t) \).

Property 3: Poisson Distribution

The number of events in any interval of length \( t \) follows a Poisson distribution:

\[ P(N(t) = k) = \frac{(\lambda t)^k e^{-\lambda t}}{k!}, \quad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots \]

Expected Value and Variance

For a Poisson process \( N(t) \) with rate \( \lambda \):

\[ E[N(t)] = \lambda t \]

\[ \text{Var}(N(t)) = \lambda t \]

The mean and variance are equal, a characteristic property of the Poisson distribution.

Interarrival Times

The time between consecutive events follows an exponential distribution:

\[ T_i \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda) \]

\[ f(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}, \quad t \geq 0 \]

This is due to the memoryless property of exponential distributions.

The Rate Parameter λ

The rate parameter \( \lambda \) is the fundamental characteristic of a Poisson process, determining both the frequency and distribution of events.

Interpretation Mathematical Expression Practical Meaning
Average rate \( \lambda = \frac{E[N(t)]}{t} \) Expected events per unit time
Instantaneous rate \( \lambda = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{P(N(\Delta t) = 1)}{\Delta t} \) Probability intensity at any moment
Reciprocal of mean waiting time \( \lambda = \frac{1}{E[T]} \) Higher λ means shorter waits between events

Units and interpretation: If time is measured in seconds and \( \lambda = 5 \), we expect 5 events per second on average. If \( \lambda = 0.1 \), we expect 1 event every 10 seconds. The parameter \( \lambda \) scales linearly with expected counts: over interval \( [0, t] \), expect \( \lambda t \) events.

In cybersecurity: A network intrusion detection system might observe attack attempts at rate \( \lambda = 0.5 \) per hour, meaning an average of one attack every 2 hours, or 12 attacks per day.

Simulation Analysis

Convergence to the Poisson Process

The discrete approximation converges to a true Poisson process as the number of subintervals \( n \to \infty \). This convergence can be understood through several perspectives.

Limit theorem: Let \( X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n \) be independent Bernoulli trials with \( P(X_i = 1) = \lambda T / n \). Then as \( n \to \infty \), the sum \( \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i \) converges in distribution to \( \text{Poisson}(\lambda T) \).

Why it works: The Poisson distribution arises as the limit of binomial distributions \( \text{Binomial}(n, p) \) when \( n \to \infty \) and \( p \to 0 \) such that \( np = \lambda \) remains constant. Our simulation implements exactly this limiting process.

Practical convergence: For \( n = 5000 \) and moderate \( \lambda \) (say, \( \lambda < 10 \)), the approximation error is negligible. The discrete simulation produces sample paths virtually indistinguishable from a true Poisson process.

Applications in Cybersecurity

Poisson processes model numerous phenomena in cybersecurity and network systems where events occur randomly over time.

Application Event Type Typical Rate λ Analysis Goal
Intrusion Detection Attack attempts 0.1 - 10 per hour Predict attack frequency, resource allocation
Network Traffic Packet arrivals 10³ - 10⁶ per second Queue analysis, bandwidth planning
Server Requests HTTP requests 1 - 1000 per second Load balancing, capacity planning
System Failures Component failures 0.001 - 0.1 per day Reliability analysis, maintenance scheduling
DDoS Detection Suspicious connections Variable, anomaly if λ spikes Anomaly detection, threshold setting

Detection strategy: Establish baseline rate \( \lambda_0 \) during normal operation. Monitor observed counts \( N(t) \). If \( N(t) \) significantly exceeds \( \lambda_0 t + k\sqrt{\lambda_0 t} \) (for threshold \( k \approx 3 \)), flag as anomaly.

Properties and Statistical Tests

The Poisson process has well-defined statistical properties that can be tested empirically from simulated or observed data.

Key Statistical Properties

Distribution of N(t): For fixed \( t \), \( N(t) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda t) \)

Test: Generate many realizations, construct histogram, compare to theoretical PMF

Mean-variance equality: \( E[N(t)] = \text{Var}(N(t)) = \lambda t \)

Test: Compute sample mean and variance, check if approximately equal

Interarrival times: Exponentially distributed with parameter \( \lambda \)

Test: Extract waiting times between events, fit to exponential distribution

Independent increments: Counts in disjoint intervals are independent

Test: Compute correlation between counts in non-overlapping intervals (should be near zero)
