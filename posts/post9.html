Axiomatic Foundations of Probability Theory

Interpretations, measure theory, and fundamental derivations — Daniele De Pascali 1984462

Interpretations of Probability

Probability has been interpreted in fundamentally different ways throughout history, each offering unique perspectives on uncertainty and randomness. The major interpretations reflect different philosophical approaches to what probability means.

Interpretation Core Idea Definition Key Limitation
Classical Equal likelihood \( P(A) = \frac{\text{favorable}}{\text{total}} \) Requires finite, equally likely outcomes
Frequentist Long-run frequency \( P(A) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n_A}{n} \) Cannot handle single events
Bayesian Degree of belief \( P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)} \) Subjective priors
Geometric Ratio of measures \( P(A) = \frac{\mu(A)}{\mu(\Omega)} \) Requires measurable sets

Classical interpretation is based on Laplace's principle of indifference and works for symmetric situations like dice and cards. However, it faces circular reasoning—how to define "equally likely" without already knowing probabilities?

Frequentist interpretation defines probability as relative frequency in repeated trials. It's objective and based on observable data, but cannot assign probabilities to unique, non-repeatable events.

Bayesian interpretation treats probability as degree of belief, updated with new evidence via Bayes' theorem. It can handle unique events but depends on subjective prior beliefs.

Geometric interpretation generalizes the classical approach to continuous spaces using measure theory, providing a rigorous mathematical foundation for continuous distributions.

How Axiomatic Approach Resolves Inconsistencies

Kolmogorov's axiomatic approach (1933) provides a unified mathematical framework that transcends philosophical debates. Rather than defining what probability "is," it specifies what probability must "satisfy."

Kolmogorov's Three Axioms

Axiom 1: Non-negativity

\[ P(A) \geq 0 \quad \forall A \in \mathcal{F} \]

Axiom 2: Normalization

\[ P(\Omega) = 1 \]

Axiom 3: Countable Additivity

\[ P\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P(A_i) \]

For disjoint events

Resolution of conceptual conflicts: The axioms provide unification (all interpretations must satisfy them), consistency (preventing logical contradictions), generality (working for finite, countable, and uncountable spaces), and derivability (all probability rules follow from these three axioms). Classical interpretation doesn't need to justify "equally likely" beforehand; frequentist axioms describe limiting behavior without requiring infinite repetitions; Bayesian priors are valid as long as they satisfy axioms; and the σ-algebra structure ensures only well-defined events receive probabilities.

The axiomatic approach separates mathematical structure (what all interpretations must obey) from philosophical meaning (what probability represents), allowing different interpretations to coexist while ensuring consistency.

Probability Theory and Measure Theory

Probability theory is a special case of measure theory where the total measure equals 1. This connection provides the rigorous foundation for modern probability.

The Probability Space: \( (\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P) \)

\( \Omega \) (Sample Space): Set of all possible outcomes

\( \mathcal{F} \) (σ-Algebra): Collection of measurable events (subsets to which we assign probabilities). A σ-algebra must: (1) contain \( \Omega \), (2) be closed under complements, and (3) be closed under countable unions.

\( P \) (Probability Measure): Function \( P: \mathcal{F} \to [0,1] \) satisfying Kolmogorov's axioms

Why σ-algebras matter: Not all subsets can be assigned probabilities. The σ-algebra excludes non-measurable sets, ensuring probability is well-defined.

Measure Theory Probability Theory Relationship
Measure space \( (\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mu) \) Probability space \( (\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P) \) \( P \) is a measure with \( P(\Omega) = 1 \)
Measurable set \( A \in \mathcal{F} \) Event \( A \in \mathcal{F} \) Events are measurable sets
Measurable function Random variable \( X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R} \) where \( \{\omega : X(\omega) \leq x\} \in \mathcal{F} \)
Lebesgue integral \( \int f \, d\mu \) Expected value \( E[X] \) Expectation is integration w.r.t. \( P \)

A random variable \( X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R} \) is a measurable function where \( \{\omega \in \Omega : X(\omega) \leq x\} \in \mathcal{F} \) for all \( x \in \mathbb{R} \). This ensures we can compute \( P(X \in B) \) for any Borel set \( B \subseteq \mathbb{R} \).

Deriving Subadditivity from Axioms

The subadditivity property (Boole's inequality) states that the probability of a union is at most the sum of individual probabilities. This follows directly from Kolmogorov's axioms.

Subadditivity Theorem

For events \( A_1, A_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{F} \) (not necessarily disjoint):

\[ P\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} A_i\right) \leq \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A_i) \]

Proof for two events:

Write \( A \cup B = A \cup (B \setminus A) \) as disjoint union. By Axiom 3: \( P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B \setminus A) \). Since \( B \setminus A \subseteq B \), monotonicity gives \( P(B \setminus A) \leq P(B) \). Therefore \( P(A \cup B) \leq P(A) + P(B) \).

General proof: By induction. Base case is trivial. For \( n+1 \) events, treat \( \bigcup_{i=1}^{n+1} A_i = (\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} A_i) \cup A_{n+1} \). Apply two-event case: \( P(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n+1} A_i) \leq P(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} A_i) + P(A_{n+1}) \leq \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A_i) + P(A_{n+1}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n+1} P(A_i) \).

Intuition: Summing individual probabilities counts overlaps multiple times, leading to overestimation. Equality holds if and only if events are disjoint.

Deriving Inclusion-Exclusion Principle

The inclusion-exclusion principle provides an exact formula for the probability of a union by systematically correcting for overlaps.

Inclusion-Exclusion Principle

For events \( A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n \):

\[ P\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} A_i\right) = \sum_{i} P(A_i) - \sum_{i < j} P(A_i \cap A_j) + \sum_{i < j < k} P(A_i \cap A_j \cap A_k) - \cdots + (-1)^{n+1} P(A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_n) \]

Proof for two events: Write \( A \cup B = A \cup (B \setminus A) \) disjointly. Apply additivity: \( P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B \setminus A) \). Since \( B = (B \setminus A) \cup (A \cap B) \) disjointly, \( P(B \setminus A) = P(B) - P(A \cap B) \). Substitute: \( P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) \).

Proof for three events: Apply two-event formula to \( (A \cup B) \cup C \): \( P(A \cup B \cup C) = P(A \cup B) + P(C) - P((A \cup B) \cap C) \). Expand \( P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) \). Note \( (A \cup B) \cap C = (A \cap C) \cup (B \cap C) \), so \( P((A \cup B) \cap C) = P(A \cap C) + P(B \cap C) - P(A \cap B \cap C) \). Combining gives the formula.

Pattern: Alternating signs with term \( (-1)^{k+1} \) for \( k \)-way intersections. Each element in exactly \( k \) sets contributes \( \binom{k}{1} - \binom{k}{2} + \cdots = 1 \) to the final sum, ensuring each element is counted exactly once.
